The Black Box

Before we open it up, here is the whole system in one diagram. You feed TabICLv2 a table (some training rows with labels, one test row without) and it hands you back a prediction. No training. No gradient descent. No fitting on your data. Just a forward pass.

Figure 1. TabICLv2 as a black box. Training rows go in, the test row's prediction comes out. No fine-tuning, no per-dataset training run. The same color scheme runs through every figure in this post: training rows test row (C_004) weight matrices predictions

The whole post is about what's inside that box and why it works. Let's open it.

Why Tabular Data Is Hard

Text has a fixed vocabulary. Images have a fixed pixel grid. A model trained on English can read any new English sentence; a model trained on ImageNet can process any new RGB image of the right shape. The input space is shared.

Tables have none of that. Every dataset has different columns, different scales, different types. A model trained on the Amex default-prediction table cannot look at the iris flower dataset without retraining. There is no shared "tabular vocabulary." For the last twenty years, the answer has been: train a fresh model from scratch for every new table. Gradient-boosted trees won that era by being very good at this from-scratch fitting.

What changed: TabPFN (2022) showed you can pre-train a transformer on millions of synthetic tables, and it learns a general skill of "reading a tabular context to predict a held-out row." TabPFN, TabPFNv2, TabICL, and now TabICLv2 all live in this paradigm.

The Foundation-Model Idea

Here is the paradigm shift in one diagram.

Figure 2. Classical ML fits a model to your data, so every new dataset means a new training run. TabICLv2 is pre-trained once, on a generative process that samples a fresh synthetic dataset every step. After that, any table you hand it is just context for a forward pass.

The training data for the foundation model is not real tables. It is millions of synthetic tables generated by a procedural process that we will look at in section 14. The point of pre-training is not to memorize any table (that would be useless). It is to learn the general skill of reading any tabular context and predicting a held-out row. That skill is what gets baked into the weights.

Now we open the box. From here on, everything we look at is what happens inside that black diagram in Figure 1.

The Three-Stage Architecture

TabICLv2 has three stages. The first turns each row into a vector by looking at how its features relate to each other. The second stitches those vectors together. The third is the heart of in-context learning: the test row attends across the training rows to find ones that look like it.

Figure 3. The architectural skeleton. Stage 1 (TF_col) lets features within a single row interact. Stage 2 (TF_row) compresses each row's enriched features into a single vector. Stage 3 (TF_icl) is the in-context learning step: the test row attends across the training rows. The whole pipeline runs L times in stacked layers.

Before any of these three stages can run, we need the table in a form a transformer can chew on. That means turning numbers into vectors. The next two sections handle that, and they are where the first two TabICLv2-specific ideas live.

How a Row Becomes a Vector, Part 1: Feature Grouping

Throughout the rest of this post we will use one running example. A simplified slice of the American Express default prediction dataset with five features per customer.

Figure 4. Our running example. The pattern is intentionally obvious: high balance ratio + low payment ratio + many days delinquent points to default. C_004 looks a lot like C_001 and C_003. So when the model attends across rows, it should pull most of its signal from those two, both of whom defaulted. In the real Amex dataset these features map to B_1, S_3, P_2, D_39, and R_1.

The first step is to turn each row into a sequence of vectors, one per feature. The naïve way is to embed each column independently with its own linear layer. TabICL did this. The problem: when two columns have similar distributions (say balance ratio and risk score, both bounded in [0, 1] and both leaning high for risky customers) independent embedding can produce nearly identical vectors. The model loses its ability to tell them apart. This is called representation collapse.

TabICLv2 fixes this with repeated feature grouping. Instead of embedding each column alone, every column is bundled with two of its neighbors via circular shifts: column j is grouped with columns j+1 and j+3 (mod m). For our 5-column table, that gives us five overlapping triples.

Figure 5. Repeated feature grouping on C_001. Each of the five raw features generates one triple (itself plus its +1 and +3 neighbors, modulo 5). Every triple goes through the same linear layer to produce one d-dimensional embedding. Result: still five embeddings (the count is preserved) but each one now depends on three columns, breaking the symmetry that caused representation collapse in TabICL.

The shift pattern (0, 1, 3) is chosen so that for any table with seven or more columns, no pair of features ever appears together in more than one triple. That gives the model a richer mix of contexts without redundant information.

How a Row Becomes a Vector, Part 2: Target-Aware Embedding

Now the second TabICLv2-specific trick. We need the model to know which rows are training rows (with labels) and which is the test row (without). The previous generation, TabPFNv2, handled this by appending the target as an extra column. TabICLv2 does something cleaner: it adds a target embedding directly to every feature embedding of every training row.

For C_001 (label = default), an embedding for "default" gets added to all five of its feature embeddings. Same for C_002 (label = no default) and C_003 (label = default). C_004 is the test row, so nothing is added. Every downstream layer can tell which is which by whether or not the target signal is present.

Figure 6. Target-aware embedding. The model adds a label-specific vector to every feature embedding of every training row. The test row gets nothing added, and that absence is itself a signal. The paper's ablations identify this as one of three components (alongside the Muon optimizer and QASSMax) that each contribute roughly 100 Elo on validation datasets.

Column-Wise Attention: Features Talking to Each Other

Now stage 1 actually runs. TF_col is a transformer that lets the features within a single row attend to each other. Why? Because raw values are ambiguous in isolation. payment ratio = 0.12 alone could mean a customer who recently paid down a balance. balance ratio = 0.93 AND payment ratio = 0.12 AND days delinquent = 14 together scream "this customer is in trouble." Column-wise attention lets the model learn these joint signals.

The crucial detail: this stage runs independently for every row. C_001 attends only to C_001's features. C_002 attends only to C_002's. They never interact at this stage.

Figure 7. Column-wise attention inside C_001. The five feature embeddings (after grouping and target injection) attend to each other through TF_col. The output is five enriched feature embeddings: each one now encodes not just its own value but how that value relates to the rest of the row. Technically TF_col is a Set Transformer, which uses learnable "inducing points" to keep the cost linear in the number of columns rather than quadratic.

Row Pooling: Collapsing to One Vector Per Row

After column-wise attention, each row has five enriched feature embeddings. But for stage 3 to work, every row needs to be a single vector. That is what gets compared across rows. Stage 2 (TF_row) handles the compression with a [CLS] token, the same trick BERT uses to summarize a sentence into one vector.

Figure 8. Row pooling via TF_row. A learned [CLS] token is prepended to the row's enriched feature embeddings. After a transformer pass, the [CLS] output is the row vector: one summary vector per row. This is what enters stage 3.

The Sequence of Row Vectors

Stages 1 and 2 ran four times in parallel, once for each customer. Now we have four row vectors: h_C001, h_C002, h_C003, and h_C004. Stack them. This sequence is what enters stage 3.

Figure 9. The four row vectors after stages 1 and 2. Three training rows (teal) carry their label signal through the target-aware embedding. The test row (dark cyan) does not. This is the sequence of tokens that enters stage 3.

Row-Wise ICL: The In-Context Learning Step

This is the heart of in-context learning. The test row's vector h_C004 needs to find which training rows it resembles, then pull their label signal toward itself. Transformers do this with three projections: Query, Key, and Value. Each row vector is multiplied by three learned weight matrices to produce three new vectors per row.

Figure 10. Each row vector h gets projected into three new vectors (q, k, v) by three learned weight matrices. The matrices are shared across all rows. C_004's q_004 is what we will compare against every training row's k.

First step Compute the dot product between C_004's query and every training row's key. This produces a similarity score for each candidate.

Second step Divide by √d_k (where d_k is the head dimension) to keep dot products from blowing up at high dimensions, then apply softmax to turn the scores into a probability distribution. The result is a set of attention weights that sum to 1.

Figure 12. Attention weights after softmax(scores / √d_k). The model puts most of its attention on C_001 and C_003, both defaulters. The combined attention on default-class training rows is …. In plain English: the model is paying about 94% of its attention to customers who defaulted. That signal will dominate the next step.

Third step Take a weighted sum of the value vectors using these attention weights. The result is a new vector for C_004. Its representation is now enriched by the values of the rows it most resembles. Because those rows carry the "default" target signal that was injected back in stage 1, that signal flows through and ends up dominating C_004's enriched representation.

The trick

The training labels were never an output the model is asked to predict in this single step. They were baked into the row vectors as input features, way back in target-aware embedding. By the time row-wise ICL runs, "find similar rows" and "copy their label" have already become the same operation, because similar rows literally have the label signal stored in their value vectors.

Why Q, K, V Are Three Separate Things

It looks like overkill. Three different projections of the same row vector. Here is the library analogy that makes it click. You walk into a library looking for something. The query is what you want ("books about default risk"). The key is what each book advertises on its spine ("Credit Risk Modeling," the topic in shorthand). The value is what is actually inside the book: the chapters, examples, equations.

You match your query against keys to figure out which books to pull off the shelf. Then you read the values of those books to learn something. The keys advertise; the values deliver. Separating them lets the model learn one thing for "what should match" and another for "what to extract once matched." For tabular ICL this is exactly the right structure: match rows on their features, but extract from them the label-flavored representation.

Masking

Masking does two jobs in TabICLv2, and they are easy to confuse.

Label masking is the bigger one. The test row's label is hidden from the input. C_004 enters the pipeline with no target embedding added; that is the masking. Without this, the model would learn to copy the label directly and never need to attend to anything. The whole prediction task only exists because we hide the answer.

Figure 13. The two jobs of masking. Left: C_004's label is removed from the input, which is what creates the prediction task in the first place. Right: in TF_icl, the attention matrix has C_004's position blocked with −∞ scores, so softmax sends those weights to exactly zero. No row, including C_004 itself, attends to a label that is not there.

The −∞ trick works because softmax exponentiates first. exp(−∞) = 0, so the weight on that path is precisely zero. No need to special-case anything in the math. Just inject the −∞ before the softmax and the rest of the pipeline behaves correctly.

Attention Fading and QASSMax

Now we get to TabICLv2's headline architectural contribution. Standard softmax has a problem at scale that does not show up in our four-row toy example but bites hard when you scale to real datasets. The denominator of the softmax is a sum over every row in the context. Push from 1,000 rows to 1,000,000 rows and that denominator grows roughly proportionally to n. Even if C_004's "true" similarity to one specific anchor row is enormous, the resulting attention weight gets washed out across the sea of other rows. TabICL trained on tables of up to 60K rows, and its attention started to flatten when pushed beyond that. This is called attention fading.

TabICLv2's fix is QASSMax, short for Query-Aware Scalable Softmax. The intuition is simple: scale the queries by something that grows with log(n) before computing softmax. As the dataset grows, queries get scaled up, dot-product magnitudes grow with them, and softmax stays sharp. A theoretical result shows that log(n) scaling is exactly the right rate to maintain attention sharpness as n grows.

QASSMax keeps the anchor row visibly above the noise floor across orders of magnitude. This is what lets TabICLv2 generalize to million-row tables despite being pre-trained on contexts up to 60K rows.

In TabICLv2's actual implementation, the scaling factor is not just log(n) · s but a small per-element MLP, plus a query-dependent gate. The principle is identical; the parameterization is more expressive.

Where the Weights Came From

We have spent thirteen sections looking at the architecture without saying where W_Q, W_K, W_V, the linear layers, and the QASSMax MLPs actually come from. Here is the training loop.

Figure 15. The training loop. The headline number: roughly 24.5 H100-days of pre-training. ~550K total iterations across three stages, batch size 64, with each iteration sampling a completely fresh synthetic table.

The training data is not a fixed dataset. It is a generative process, a procedural prior that samples a new synthetic table on every step. The recipe: sample a random directed acyclic graph; at each node, place a random function (MLP, tree ensemble, Gaussian process, polynomial, plateau function, or product of these); sample input vectors at the root nodes; propagate them through the graph; extract some columns as features and one as the target. Throw away datasets where the target is independent of the features. Repeat about 35 million times.

Here is the central insight that makes pre-training work at all. Because every iteration uses a different dataset, memorizing any specific one is worthless. The optimizer cannot lower loss by getting really good at one table; by next step, that table is gone forever. The only thing that consistently lowers loss across millions of distinct datasets is the general skill of reading a tabular context to predict a held-out row. That skill is what gets compressed into the weights. In meta-learning terms, the prior is the inner-loop task distribution, and the outer loop is gradient descent on the model's ability to handle that whole distribution at once.

The loss is just expected negative log-likelihood of the test row's true label, averaged over all sampled datasets. Concretely: every gradient step pushes W_Q, W_K, W_V to make the rows that share a label produce higher dot products than rows that do not, because that is what consistently makes the test-row prediction correct, regardless of what dataset we are looking at.

Stacking Layers

One pass of TF_col → TF_row → TF_icl is not enough. TabICLv2 stacks these into L layers. Early layers learn basic feature-feature interactions and rough row-row matches; deeper layers refine these into sharper, more abstract patterns.

Figure 16. The full stack. Each layer takes the previous layer's row vectors, runs them through the same three-stage architecture, and produces refined row vectors for the next layer. The W_Q, W_K, W_V matrices are different per layer; every layer learns its own attention patterns.

If you remember one thing

TabICLv2 takes a table, reads it once, predicts. The "training data" lives in the context window, not the weights. The weights store the general skill of reading tabular context, a skill it learned from tens of millions of synthetic tables, none of which it ever saw twice.

What We Glossed Over

A few things were simplified to keep the diagrams readable. None of them change the picture above; they are the kind of details that turn into footnotes once you have the main idea.

Every QKV walkthrough used a single attention head. The actual model uses 8 heads in both TF_col and TF_icl, with their outputs concatenated. The intuition is unchanged; you can think of each head as a separate "match on this kind of similarity, extract this kind of value" pattern, learned independently. The set of heads gives the layer multiple ways to look at the same row sequence at once.

When we said TF_col is a Set Transformer, that is more than a label. The reason it is a Set Transformer rather than a vanilla one is the use of inducing points: a small fixed-size set of learnable queries that all input tokens attend to first, then the output is read back from the inducing points. This makes attention cost linear in the number of features instead of quadratic, which is why TabICLv2 stays fast even on tables with hundreds of columns.

We described classification throughout. For regression, TabICLv2 swaps the classification head for one that predicts 999 quantiles, trained with pinball loss. The architecture above stays identical. This choice is unusual: most tabular foundation models either bin the target into a categorical or predict a single point estimate. TabICLv2's quantile head gives you a full predictive distribution essentially for free, including tail probabilities and confidence intervals.

We also assumed at most 10 classes. TabICLv2 was pre-trained with up to 10, and for problems with more it uses a trick called mixed-radix ensembling. The class label gets decomposed into multiple "digits" in different bases (each at most 10), the model runs once per digit, and the per-digit predictions are recombined. It is its own small idea worth a separate post.

Finally, the pretraining setup. Three stages, with dataset sizes growing from 1,024 rows in stage 1 to up to 60K rows in stage 3. The optimizer is Muon rather than the AdamW you might expect, which lets the model use much higher learning rates (8e-4 in stage 1, versus 1e-4 for AdamW in TabICL). Cautious weight decay is applied (decay only when the update and parameter agree in sign) so beneficial gradient directions are not interfered with. Total cost: about 24.5 H100-days, which is cheap by foundation-model standards.

The synthetic prior is its own appendix. Eight types of random functions, multiple random graph topologies, sampling correlated scalars, dataset-level filtering. The prior turns out to be the single highest-impact ablation: training the v2 architecture on the v1 prior fails outright. Most of the engineering effort behind TabICLv2 lives in this generative process.

For all of these, read the paper. The architecture is in §3, pretraining in §4, the synthetic data engine in §5, ablations in §7, and the quantile-distribution math in Appendix I.
